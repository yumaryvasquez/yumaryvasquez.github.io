  I am studying endemic Hawaiian leafhoppers (Nesophrosyne) which generally maintains a relationship with the symbionts, Candidatus Sulcia muelleri and Candidatus Nasuia deltocephalinicola. These symbionts are housed in a specialized host organelle (bacteriome) and further inhabit specialized host cells (bacteriocytes). They also provide 10 missing essential amino acids that are limited in the host's diet. Both Sulcia (190kb) and Nasuia (112kb) have limited gene sets because genetic drift continues to cause an elevated strain of slightly deleterious mutations. Insect hosts continue to adapt to compensate for symbiotic gene loss. Consequently, hosts could potentially have a lower fitness in the process and thus implement symbiont replacement as the only way to rectify the loss. 
  
  My project looks to answer three questions pertaining multi-partner symbiosis:
  
Question 1: How do obligate symbiont genomes evolve? 
Question 2: How do hosts co-evolve to maintain their symbionts?
Question 3: How do hosts reestablish multi-partner symbiosis when the ancient symbiont is replaced?
